### The Cisco SD-WAN Manager cluster — a clean datapoint on KEV editorial lag

The three Cisco SD-WAN Manager CVEs deserve a separate section because they resolve one of our open questions:

- **CVE-2026-20122** (vManage arbitrary file overwrite via API) and **CVE-2026-20128** (passwords stored in recoverable format) had their active exploitation publicly confirmed by Cisco PSIRT on **2026-03-05** (Help Net Security, SOCRadar, The Hacker News all reported independently that Cisco updated the advisory to flag in-the-wild exploitation). Third parties observed web shell deployments and high-volume exploitation attempts against the vManage API.
- **KEV addition: 2026-04-20.** That is a **46-day gap from confirmed-exploitation-in-the-wild to KEV listing.**

This is important for our "PoC → KEV" heuristic work this week. It suggests that CISA's editorial threshold does not merely require *any* in-the-wild exploitation evidence — it requires evidence CISA can vet against U.S.-federal-sector telemetry, and then still passes through a significant editorial queue. A 46-day delay for a Cisco product with named-vendor-confirmed exploitation sets a plausible upper bound on the horizon for our open watch-list items with weaker exploitation evidence (Marimo, n8n, BlueHammer). **Translation: expect Marimo to either land on KEV in the next 2–4 weeks or to age out entirely.**

Counter-read: these three Cisco CVEs had exploitation confirmed by Cisco itself, not by an independent CSA partner. CISA may wait for independent federal-civilian confirmation. If that's the mechanism, delays for community-reported exploitation (Sysdig on Marimo, public-PoC-only on n8n) could be even longer.

### Cisco SD-WAN cluster and the probable-participant-self-scan question

Cisco is a Glasswing participant, and the batch pattern (three related SD-WAN Manager CVEs released together, all HTTP-parsing-adjacent on the management plane) is the kind of clustering we have been flagging as consistent with an automated-audit sweep. **However, these three CVEs fail qualification criterion (3) — "no known attacker exploitation before disclosure"** — because Cisco confirmed active exploitation in early March 2026. So per our current rules, they do *not* join the probable-participant-self-scan table. **No config.json update under `glasswing_targets.probable_participant_cves.entries` this run.**

This is an important principled limit on the probable-self-scan framework: automated-AI-audit clustering and pre-disclosure attacker exploitation are not mutually exclusive in reality, but they *are* mutually exclusive under our current qualification criteria. If we continue to see Cisco-sized clusters that happen to also be actively exploited, we may need to consider a separate "cluster pattern despite pre-disclosure exploitation" bucket. Not adding one yet — single data point — but flagging the design question.

### OpenClaw cluster — another AI-stack vulnerability flood

Today's NVD pull (38 total CVEs published 2026-04-21 as of 04:12Z, plus back-population to 2026-04-20) shows a **~12-CVE cluster in OpenClaw** (IDs 41285 through 41331 range published together, plus CVE-2026-35570). This tracks with the TryOpenClaw and OpenClawAI writeups describing "Nine CVEs in Four Days" in March 2026 — OpenClaw, an open-source AI agent platform, appears to be getting the same systematic-audit attention n8n, Marimo, and FastGPT have drawn.

Notable OpenClaw CVEs in today's pull include **CVE-2026-41329** (sandbox bypass via heartbeat context and parameter escalation, CVSS 9.9), **CVE-2026-41296** (sandbox escape via TOCTOU race in remote filesystem, CVSS 8.8), and **CVE-2026-41303** (authorization bypass in Discord approval commands, CVSS 8.8). These are not strict HTTP-parsing bugs in the classical web-server sense, but most are reachable across HTTP/WebSocket channels, so they sit on the same attack surface we care about.

I am **not** adding OpenClaw CVEs to the watch list this run. Rationale: (a) we already have Marimo and n8n as representative AI-stack HTTP-parsing entries and (b) the thesis test we care about (AI-tooling CVEs reach KEV within the predicted window) does not require widening the sample — it requires watching the ones we already have. Adding more would inflate the watch-list-hit-rate denominator without generating new information per entry.

**Observation worth restating:** The AI-stack-products-as-target pattern continues to look far more robust than the Glasswing-target-products-as-AI-source pattern. That is, AI-powered tooling products are demonstrably being exploited right now (Marimo 662 events, n8n unauth RCE, OpenClaw sandbox escapes). Glasswing-target libraries (Firefox, wolfSSL, NGINX, FreeBSD, OpenSSL) being exploited because Mythos found them remains a hypothesis with zero in-KEV data points. The AI-security story of April 2026 is "new AI-stack products have soft HTTP surfaces," not "AI finds bugs at scale."

That gives the Mythos-attribution-to-KEV pipeline a very long reasonable window to still be consistent with "Mythos exploitation is happening but we cannot see it yet." If a wolfSSL/Firefox/FreeBSD CVE attributed to Mythos were exploited within the next 4 weeks, we would not expect to see it in KEV for another 6–8 weeks after that. **Realistic earliest date for Mythos-era exploitation evidence in KEV: late May 2026.** That is later than my stated 2026-05-15 deadline for "volume falsification" and it means the exploitation falsification window is effectively closed until July-ish.

This cuts both ways. It extends the period during which absence-of-evidence is not evidence-of-absence for Mythos exploitation. It also reduces the near-term information value of the watch list, because the true KEV queue is operating at 6-week latency rather than the days-to-week latency the security-marketing machine implies.

## Thesis Challenge (Non-HTTP Library Exploits)

**No new counter-examples today.** Searched across the 2026-04-20 KEV batch (all 8 HTTP-parsing-adjacent), the 2026-04-21 NVD pull (38 entries), and the past-week disclosure feed. **Zero library or framework CVEs reached KEV via a purely non-HTTP vector this cycle.** Today's near-misses:

- **CVE-2026-33827 (Windows TCP/IP RCE, CVSS 9.8):** Still not in KEV 6 days post-disclosure. Classic candidate if it ever goes — pure network-stack RCE with wormable potential. The best single candidate we have for a thesis-disconfirming KEV add.
- **CVE-2026-41329 (OpenClaw sandbox bypass, CVSS 9.9):** Reachable via HTTP/WebSocket, so not a thesis counter-example. Another AI-stack datapoint.
- **CVE-2026-41285 (OpenBSD slaacd/rad ICMPv6 loop):** Non-HTTP vector but also low severity (CVSS 4.3) and no exploitation pathway to code execution — not a plausible KEV candidate. Interesting because it's a pure network-stack bug found through what reads like a fuzzing campaign. If OpenBSD-layer CVEs start appearing in KEV, this becomes more interesting.

Absence-of-counter-examples still reads as weak support for the thesis, with the standard detection-bias caveat: non-HTTP library exploitation (file-parsing libs via email attachments, crypto libs via stored credentials, serialization libs via message queues) is structurally hard to observe. We should expect the thesis to look falsely strong in our data even if the real-world lift is only 2x instead of 3-6x. No tables updated this run.

## Big Picture

**What today tells us that yesterday didn't, in two paragraphs.** First, the KEV pipeline is not broken — it is just slow. The four-day dry spell ended on a batch whose editorial thread is clearly "Q1-to-Q2 catalog housekeeping plus confirmed Cisco SD-WAN exploitation." Ten of April's 22 KEV entries are pre-2026 CVEs, which means CISA's queue is heavily weighted toward back-catalog work right now. That is itself a signal: if CISA's bandwidth is going to catalog housekeeping, that is bandwidth that isn't going to new-exploitation triage, and the KEV lag for 2026-disclosed CVEs should be longer than it has been historically. This makes me move my Marimo KEV-add forecast **back out to 30 days** rather than 7.

Second, the 46-day exploitation-to-KEV gap for Cisco SD-WAN is the most concrete datapoint we have on CISA's editorial velocity under current conditions, and it reframes the entire Mythos-falsification question. My earlier 2026-05-15 "if no Glasswing volume surge, the volume thesis is falsified" deadline still stands for *volume*. But the exploitation-half of the thesis now has a plausible 6–8 week lag baked in from discovery → exploitation telemetry → KEV confirmation. Even if Mythos were finding exploitable bugs right now, we would not reasonably expect to see them in KEV until July at the earliest, which conveniently aligns with Anthropic's planned July disclosure window. **The absence of Mythos-attributable KEV entries today is completely consistent with both a real Mythos exploitation effect and a null Mythos exploitation effect.** The data cannot distinguish these hypotheses for another 8–12 weeks.

**Is Glasswing/Mythos changing the vulnerability landscape yet?** Still no at the aggregate level, but with an important caveat: the window in which we can reasonably expect to see evidence has moved out by ~6 weeks based on today's KEV-latency data. If mid-May arrives with no visible Glasswing volume surge, the volume thesis is falsified. If July arrives with no Mythos-attributable KEV entries, the exploitation thesis is falsified. We are now explicitly holding the exploitation thesis on a longer leash than I thought was justified as recently as two days ago.

**Is AI adoption changing the landscape yet?** Yes, through attack-surface expansion. Marimo, n8n, FastGPT, and now OpenClaw have collectively contributed more CVEs in the 2026-April window than wolfSSL, Firefox, NGINX, and FreeBSD combined (Glasswing's own target list). If the "AI is changing cybersecurity" headline were to be rewritten for accuracy based on today's data, it would be "AI-*product* adoption is generating novel attack surface faster than AI-*discovery* tools are finding bugs in legacy code." Both may matter long-term, but one of them is visibly happening this week and the other is a future contingency.

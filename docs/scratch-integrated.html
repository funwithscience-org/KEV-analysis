Cross-Framework Patch Periodicity

How four production stacks see CVE pressure across a 12-month window and a 7-year arc, scored against four triage strategies — patch-everything, NP+DI, NP+DI + AI scan, and a hacker S+A discriminator — and cross-checked with WAF-defensibility on the bugs that actually got exploited.

1. The 12-month per-framework view

Four production-shaped manifests across a single year (Apr 2025 – Apr 2026): Spring Boot 48 deps, Node.js/Express 45 deps, Django/DRF 40 deps, Netty 7 deps. For each, four triage strategies: patch-all-criticals-and-highs, NP+DI (network parser × direct injection CWE), NP+DI + AI scan (rescues for events whose surface CWE underweights the primitive), and the hacker S+A discriminator (default-config × network-edge × primitive-direct, with the auth-missing co-tag rule).

2. Periodicity: silence punctuated by bursts (12-month view)

Averages mislead here. Neither NP+DI nor hacker S+A produces evenly spaced triggers — both produce silence punctuated by bursts. Spring had 5.5 months of NP+DI silence, then two hits in a week. Node had 10 months of NP+DI silence, then two hits in 29 days. The hacker filter extends those silences further on Spring and Django because its bar is stricter, but compresses on Node because all three of its hits cluster into Q1 2026.

3. Why 12 months is too short

The 12-month per-framework view has zero exploited CVEs in three of the four manifests — Node, Django, and Netty all hit 0 KEV/MSF entries in the window. Spring has one (CVE-2026-34197 ActiveMQ Jolokia, late in the window). That's a clean coverage test — every strategy looks like it "works" because there's almost nothing to miss — but the discrimination question is unanswered.

To force the strategies to differentiate, we widen the scope:

  • A 7-year window instead of 12 months — enough exploit ground truth to actually score effectiveness.
  • An actual production estate manifest with version sprawl — Spring Boot 2.x estate with 60 unique packages across 176 version-pinned entries, not a synthetic 48-package framework view.
  • Effective coverage via floor sweep, not just direct catch — a routine rebuild applies the latest version of every dependency, which means a long-TTE exploit gets effectively patched if any other strategy trigger fires during the window.
  • Burst vs silence operational pattern, not just averaged "patch events per year".

4. The production manifest (7-year scope)

Scope: actual deployed Spring Boot 2.x production estate — 60 unique packages across 176 version-pinned entries (multi-version snapshot with real-world version sprawl). 223 critical/high CVEs over 7 years; 13 actually exploited.

What's in the manifest:

  • Spring 4.x AND 5.x (spring-web 4.1.6 through 5.3.31, 13 versions; spring-beans 4.1.6 through 5.3.13, 8 versions), Spring Boot 2.2-2.7, Spring Security 4.x and 5.x, Spring Integration, Spring WS
  • Tomcat embed 8.5 + 9.0 (5 versions), Tomcat coyote 6.x
  • Both log4j lines: log4j:log4j 1.2.6/1.2.15/1.2.17 (long-EOL but deployed) AND log4j-core 2.8.2 (Log4Shell-vulnerable). Logback also present.
  • ActiveMQ activemq-all 5.12.0 (broker+client), Apache MINA, Apache SSHD, Hazelcast cluster wire protocol
  • Apache CXF (api + databinding-aegis), Spring WS, dom4j 1.x and 2.x, Apache XMLBeans
  • Deserialization gadget classics: commons-collections 3.2.x, XStream 1.4.x (3 versions), Jackson 1.x (jackson-mapper-asl) and Jackson 2.x (databind 2.3.1 through 2.9.8, 7 versions), SnakeYAML 1.3 through 1.33 (9 versions), Apache BCEL 6.3

  • Build tooling: Maven core/settings/shared-utils/surefire, plexus-archiver/utils, Apache Ivy, WireMock, Springfox Swagger UI

7. Averages lie — burst vs silence pattern

The averaged "patch events per year" obscures the real defender experience. During busy quarters (≥10 C/H disclosures) every reasonable strategy fires multiple times — the filter's discrimination is muted. During quiet quarters strategies diverge sharply on whether to fire at all. The savings concentrate in quiet periods, not the average.

Hacker S+A produces silence for 17 of 31 quarters (55%). NP+DI+AI scan: 6 silent quarters. Patch-all C+H: never silent. The averaged overhead numbers describe the same total work but mis-locate when it lands. The hacker discriminator's value is "long stretches of nothing, then a real fire when it matters."
The flip side: during the bursts, no strategy buys you out of the work. 2020-Q2 (29 events), 2021-Q4 (20 events Log4Shell+Spring Security), 2022-Q4 (Text4Shell+SnakeYAML+Tomcat smuggling) — the team is in emergency mode regardless. The hacker's value isn't fewer emergencies during bursts; it's not having emergencies during the 17 quiet quarters when the comparator and patch-criticals are still demanding attention.

8. Effectiveness via floor sweep

Direct catch is the strict measure: did the strategy fire on the trigger date for the exploited package? But that's not how a real estate works. Once a strategy fires, the rebuild it kicks off applies the latest version of every dependency, not just the trigger. So a long-TTE exploit gets effectively patched if any other strategy trigger fires during the time-to-exploit window.

The exposure ceiling for any specific exploit becomes "until the next strategy trigger fires," not "until the strategy catches this specific event." See the build-mechanics page for the underlying argument.

The hacker discriminator's value concentrates on WAF-medium and WAF-hostile events. Of the 9 events the hacker S+A catches, 1 is WAF-hostile (Ghostcat AJP), 6 are WAF-medium (the entire deserialization cluster: XStream, log4j ×2, SnakeYAML, Tomcat partial-PUT, ActiveMQ Jolokia), and 2 are WAF-friendly (Spring4Shell, Text4Shell). The hacker filter elevates exactly the bug classes a perimeter cannot meaningfully time-shift — which is the operational reason "patch fast" is not optional for these.
The 4 WAF-friendly events are where the comparator and the hacker overlap most cleanly. Spring4Shell and Text4Shell are caught by both NP+DI+AI and Hacker; the Tomcat HTTP PUT pair (2017) is missed by both (out-of-scope CWE-434). For these, an emergency WAF rule plus a normal-cadence rebuild is sufficient — the patch can wait for the next planned window. The remaining 9 (medium + hostile) are where the hacker's "patch this now" verdict holds even with a WAF in front.
Operator/defender symmetry. The same axis that tells an operator "this campaign has a long window because perimeters can't close it" tells a defender "no perimeter substitute exists, you have to deploy the patch." The bugs the operator most wants are also the bugs the defender most needs to patch fast. The hacker discriminator is reading that signal one step earlier than the WAF telemetry would.

10. Where attack surface concentrates vs where it doesn't

  • Web servers + deserialization libraries dominate the exploited set — 11 of 13 exploits hit Tomcat (5), XStream (2), log4j-core (2), Spring (1), ActiveMQ broker (1). Remaining 2 are utility-library evaluator bugs (commons-text, SnakeYAML).
  • Build tooling: 9 C/H events over 7 years, zero exploited. Maven / plexus / ivy / surefire packages contribute path traversal, command injection, dep-confusion, XXE — all real bugs but none in KEV/MSF/EDB. Both NP+DI+AI and Hacker S+A correctly downgrade them. Clean validation that the discriminators aren't burning patch effort on the build cluster.
  • Scripting runtimes (Groovy / JRuby / Jython): ~12 events, also zero exploited. Same pattern — real bugs, not network-edge production runtime.
  • 2 unrecoverable events: Tomcat HTTP PUT file upload from 2017 (CWE-434, deliberately out of NP+DI scope and hacker discriminator B/C-tiers file uploads). Patch-all is the only strategy that catches them.

11. Synthesis

Effectiveness headline: NP+DI + AI scan and Hacker S+A both reach 9/13 = 69% direct effectiveness and 11/13 = 85% effective coverage once floor-sweep rescues are counted. They catch different 9 events — the comparator owns the famous-bug + auth-rescue cluster; the hacker owns the deserialization + utility-evaluator cluster. Their union catches 11/13 directly. The remaining 2 (Tomcat HTTP PUT 2017) are out-of-scope by design.
Periodicity headline: Hacker S+A produces 17 silent quarters of 31 (55%) with 436-day longest silence; NP+DI + AI scan produces 6 silent quarters (19%) with 285-day longest silence. The averaged "X patches per year" hides the real defender experience — the savings concentrate in quiet stretches between bursts.
WAF headline: Of the 13 exploited events, 4 are WAF-friendly (sprint window), 7 are WAF-medium (days-to-weeks), and 1 is WAF-hostile (perimeter cannot help). The hacker S+A catches 7 of the 8 medium+hostile events — exactly where a perimeter cannot meaningfully buy time. The 4 WAF-friendly events are those where the comparator and the hacker overlap most cleanly: an emergency CRS rule + scheduled patch is sufficient. The 8 medium+hostile events are where the hacker's "patch this now" verdict holds even with a WAF in front.
Limit case (covered separately on the evergreen page): the floor-sweep model assumes "rebuild → fix appears." For Spring 5.x post-EOL events (Aug 2024+), Broadcom moved patches behind a commercial support paywall — Spring 5.x line gets fixes only via paid contract, not via OSS rebuild. 7 such CVEs accumulated so far, 0 exploited; lifecycle debt accumulating but not yet translating to incident.

Methodology and sources

12-month manifests. Spring Boot, Node.js/Express, Django/DRF, and Netty production-shaped dependency lists, queried against OSV for critical/high CVEs published Apr 2025 – Apr 2026. NP and DI flags come from the canonical classifier rules — NP if the package's primary purpose is processing untrusted input that arrives over the network or drives security decisions; DI if the CWE is in the widened injection / auth-bypass set (78, 77, 89, 94, 95, 116, 287, 289, 306, 502, 611, 693, 863, 917, 918, 1336, and others). Hacker tier assignments use the default-config × network-edge × primitive-direct discriminator with the auth-missing co-tag rule.

7-year production manifest. An actual deployed Spring Boot 2.x estate — 60 unique packages across 176 version-pinned entries reflecting real-world version sprawl (Spring 4.x and 5.x lines side by side, multiple Tomcat/Jackson/SnakeYAML versions, both log4j:log4j 1.x and log4j-core 2.x, etc.). 223 critical/high CVEs returned from OSV; 13 of those have exploitation evidence in KEV ∪ Metasploit ∪ ExploitDB.

Patch-event clustering. Triggers within 7 days of each other count as a single patch event, matching the convention used elsewhere in this analysis: a defender already in an emergency rebuild absorbs the next CVE in the same forced march.

Floor-sweep effectiveness. Per the build-mechanics page, every emergency rebuild applies the latest version of every dependency, so a long-TTE exploit is effectively patched if any other strategy trigger fires during the time-to-exploit window. Direct catches and floor-sweep rescues are accounted separately. Effectiveness is the rate of exploited events caught; efficiency is the patch-event-to-exploit-caught overhead ratio.

WAF-defensibility tags. Sourced from the WAF-aware operator pass on the 175-event Java/Spring blinded ranking. WAF-FRIENDLY = signature-able payload, generic OWASP rules cover, emergency rule ships within hours. WAF-MEDIUM = signature exists but evadable. WAF-HOSTILE = wrong protocol entirely or attack indistinguishable from legitimate traffic.

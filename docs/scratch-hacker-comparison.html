Patch efficiency: 12 months vs 7 years

Two clean tables, identical strategies, different time scopes — does the discriminator survive scaling out?

How we got here

  1. The 12-month synthetic backtest worked. NP+DI alone caught 5 of 8 in-scope exploited CVEs at day zero against a four-ecosystem manifest. EPSS ≥ 0.10 caught 1 of 8 on day one and didn't reach 8/8 until "eventually."
  2. An AI safety-net scan closed the structural gap. The 3 NP+DI misses were all CWE-misclassification cases (Ghostcat tagged CWE-269 instead of CWE-22; ActiveMQ Jolokia tagged CWE-20 instead of CWE-94; Tomcat partial PUT tagged CWE-44+502 instead of being recognized as a chained primitive). An automated CWE-validation pass on NP-but-not-DI advisories catches all 3 on day zero. NP+DI + AI scan: 8/8 with the same speed advantage over EPSS.
  3. We extended the backtest to seven years across multiple frameworks (Spring/Java + Django/Python combined manifest) to test whether the 12-month "perfect catch" framing survives at scale. 356 critical/high events, 15 actually exploited.
  4. An adversarial hacker persona, blind to actual exploitation outcomes, ranked vulnerabilities by attack value (S = drop everything, A = strong campaign candidate, B/C/D = lower priority). The hacker's discriminator was default-config × network-edge × primitive-direct — derived independently from bug shape, not from CWE-set membership.
  5. Hacker S+A turned out to be both the most efficient (fewest unnecessary patches) and safest (fewest missed exploits) review methodology at 7-year scale. 14 of 15 actual exploits caught (93% recall) at 70 patches over 7 years — vs NP+DI's 7/15 at 66 patches.

The 12-month window can't really differentiate strategies because N is too small. With only 2 exploits in window, recall snaps to 50% or 100%. The published "perfect catch" framing was always going to look this way at single-window scale — every reasonable strategy (criticals, NP+DI, AI scan, Hacker S+A) catches 1 or 2 of 2. The differentiation only shows up at multi-window scale.

"AI scan" here is the existing implementation: CWE re-validation for NP-but-not-DI advisories (catches CWE-misclassification cases like Ghostcat / Tomcat PUT / Jolokia). It does NOT currently re-classify package roles (commons-text, SnakeYAML are role=OTHER), expand DI to include CWE-502 deserialization, recognize CWE-640 unicode auth bypass, or assess image-library memory corruption. That's why it doesn't catch the 4 events between AI scan (10) and Hacker S+A (14). Extending AI scan to those four categories would close the gap.

The synthesis

The 12-month story (Table 1) is the published one and it survives scrutiny on its own terms. NP+DI catches 1 of 2, AI scan catches 2 of 2, hacker catches 2 of 2. Everything except NP+DI alone hits 100% recall. The differentiation among "good" strategies is about flag count: hacker S+A flags 12, AI scan 15, criticals 6 — all manageable at this scale.
The 7-year story (Table 2) is what shows the ordering. Hacker S+A is the clear winner: 93% recall at 5.0x oracle overhead. The next-best on either axis is NP+DI + AI scan (67% recall, 6.9x). The "patch criticals only" baseline beats NP+DI alone on both axes — a meaningful finding, since NP+DI was supposed to be more discriminating than blunt severity.

Counter-argument worth holding

  • The hacker exercise is a methodology validation, not a deployable product. Running an LLM-based hacker persona on every CVE batch is expensive and prompt-design-sensitive. The operational artifact is an AI scan extended to encode the hacker's discriminator as programmatic checks (auth-missing CWE co-tag rule, package-role-aware NP for utility libraries, CWE-502 deserialization promotion on network-facing components, native-C-library memory corruption flagging). That extension would close the 4-event gap between current AI scan (10/15) and Hacker S+A (14/15).
  • 15 exploited events is small N. The Hacker 14/15 vs NP+DI 7/15 gap is meaningful but flip 2-3 boundary events and headline numbers shift ~5pp.
  • Right-censoring affects 2024-2026 events. If recent CVEs accumulate exploitation evidence by 2027-2028, every strategy's recall improves; relative ordering should hold but absolute numbers shift.
  • Per-manifest variance. Java alone (175 events, 11 exploits) shows NP+DI at 5/11 = 45% — same shape as combined. Django's contribution (4 exploits, mostly from auth-boundary and image-library bugs that NP+DI structurally doesn't cover) drives part of Hacker S+A's relative advantage. Pure-Java estate would see similar but less dramatic numbers.

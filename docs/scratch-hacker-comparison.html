NP+DI vs EPSS vs Hacker Persona

Single 12-month window vs. seven 12-month windows — does the filter survive scaling out?

How we got here

  1. The 12-month synthetic backtest worked. NP+DI alone caught 5 of 8 in-scope exploited CVEs at day zero against a four-ecosystem manifest. EPSS ≥ 0.10 caught 1 of 8 on day one and didn't reach 8/8 until "eventually."
  2. An AI safety-net scan closed the structural gap. The 3 NP+DI misses were all CWE-misclassification cases (Ghostcat tagged CWE-269 instead of CWE-22; ActiveMQ Jolokia tagged CWE-20 instead of CWE-94; Tomcat partial PUT tagged CWE-44+502 instead of being recognized as a chained primitive). An automated CWE-validation pass on NP-but-not-DI advisories catches all 3 on day zero. NP+DI + AI scan: 8/8 with the same speed advantage over EPSS.
  3. We extended the backtest to seven years across multiple frameworks (Spring/Java + Django/Python) to test whether the 12-month "perfect catch" framing survives at scale. Total exploited universe: 15 events.
  4. An adversarial hacker persona, blind to actual exploitation outcomes, ranked vulnerabilities by attack value (S = drop everything, A = strong campaign candidate, B/C/D = lower priority). The hacker's discriminator was default-config × network-edge × primitive-direct — derived independently from the bug shape, not from CWE-set membership.
  5. Hacker S+A turned out to be both the most efficient (fewest unnecessary patches) and safest (fewest missed exploits) review methodology — recall 14 of 15 actual exploits while flagging far fewer events than NP+DI ∪ AI scan extended to the same recall.

"AI scan" here is the existing implementation (CWE re-validation for NP-but-not-DI advisories — catches CWE-misclassification cases like Ghostcat / Tomcat PUT / Jolokia). It does NOT currently re-classify package roles (commons-text, SnakeYAML are role=OTHER in the manifest), expand DI to include CWE-502 deserialization, recognize CWE-640 unicode auth bypass, or assess image-library memory corruption with shared-C-library shapes — that's why it doesn't catch the 5 events between AI scan (10) and Hacker S+A (14). Extending AI scan to those four categories would close the gap.

The synthesis

Hacker S+A is the safest review methodology at 7-year scale. 14 of 15 actual exploits caught (93% recall) at lower flag-volume than NP+DI alone produces. The single miss is the same one across both NP+DI and the hacker (Tomcat CGI Windows-only) — neither approach accidentally caught it; both downgraded it on the same narrow-precondition reasoning.
NP+DI ∪ Hacker S+A reaches 100% recall. NP+DI catches CVE-2019-0232 via the CWE-78 + Tomcat-NP combo; Hacker S+A catches the 8 events NP+DI misses. Union flags ~60 events over 7 years to catch all 15 — about 5.45× the post-hoc oracle minimum, vs. ~16× for "patch every C/H." That's the operational ceiling on a triage filter that doesn't have access to exploitation evidence.

Counter-argument worth holding

  • The hacker exercise is part of the validation methodology, not a deployable product. An LLM-based hacker persona is expensive to run on every CVE batch and depends on prompt design. The operational fix is to encode the hacker's discriminator as programmatic checks (auth-missing CWE co-tag rule, package-role-aware NP, CWE-misclassification re-validation, native-C-library memory corruption flagging). That's an extension of the AI scan, not a replacement.
  • Right-censoring affects 2024-2026 events. If recent CVEs accumulate exploitation evidence by 2027-2028, NP+DI and AI-scan recall will both improve; the relative ordering should hold but absolute numbers shift.
  • 15 exploited events over 7 years is small N. The hacker's 14/15 vs NP+DI's 7/15 is a meaningful gap but not statistically conclusive — flip 2-3 events on the boundaries and the headline numbers move ~5pp. The directional finding (hacker S+A > NP+DI alone in recall, both < oracle in precision) is robust; the precise multipliers are wide.
  • Hacker S+A wasn't validated as cross-ecosystem-portable in the same way. Java showed training-from-Java helped; Django (separate test) showed neither training nor deliberation improved over an untrained baseline. The cross-manifest finding is in HACKER-INTERVENTIONS-CROSS-MANIFEST-SYNTHESIS.md.

Operational reading

Three tiers of confidence the data supports:

  • Tier 1 — Drop everything (Hacker S): 5 events across 7 years; 100% precision both rounds. These are the unconditional one-shot RCEs (Log4j, Spring4Shell-class, ActiveMQ OpenWire-class).
  • Tier 2 — This week (Hacker S+A union with NP+DI): ~60 events across 7 years; 100% recall on actual exploitation. The operational policy is the union, not either alone.
  • Tier 3 — Regular cadence: everything else; 0 of the 15 exploits in this set landed below A-tier in either filter.

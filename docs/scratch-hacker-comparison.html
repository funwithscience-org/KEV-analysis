Real-world Java patch efficiencySpring Boot 48 + IBM MQ

Hacker S+A vs NP+DI+AI scan as comparator, single-window vs scaled-out

Scope: actual Spring Boot 3.x dependency tree (48 packages) plus IBM MQ 9.x. This is the represented enterprise estate, not an inflated supplemental view. We removed log4j (Spring Boot uses Logback by default), XStream, and ActiveMQ broker/client/core from earlier analyses — those packages weren't actually deployed in this estate, even though their CVEs propped up the apparent NP+DI catch rate. IBM MQ 9.x added but contributes zero events: zero OSV C/H entries, zero KEV mentions of IBM MQ in window (one 2015 IBM WebSphere AS hit is unrelated and out of window).

Comparator changed: NP+DI + AI scan, not raw NP+DI. The operational baseline is the production deployment with the AI-scan safety-net, since raw NP+DI alone has known CWE-misclassification gaps that the AI scan is designed to close. We still show raw NP+DI for context but it's not the bar to beat.

Net manifest: 145 critical/high CVEs over 7 years; 6 actually exploited.

How we got here

  1. The 12-month synthetic backtest worked. NP+DI catches ~5 trigger dates per year on the Spring Boot 48-package manifest. Within any single 12-month window, almost every reasonable strategy looks indistinguishable — small sample size hides differentiation.
  2. An AI safety-net scan closes the visible gaps. CWE-misclassification cases (Ghostcat tagged CWE-269 instead of CWE-22; Tomcat partial PUT tagged CWE-44+502) get caught by an automated CWE-validation pass on NP-but-not-DI advisories. NP+DI + AI scan is the operational comparator because it's what's deployed.
  3. We extended the backtest to a real 7-year window across the 48-package Spring Boot manifest to test whether the 12-month "perfect catch" framing survives at scale.
  4. An adversarial hacker persona, blind to actual exploitation outcomes, ranked vulnerabilities by attack value (S = drop everything, A = strong campaign candidate, B/C/D = lower priority). The hacker's discriminator was default-config × network-edge × primitive-direct — derived independently from CVE structure on disclosure day, not from CWE-set membership.
  5. Hacker S+A is both the most efficient AND safest review methodology on the 7-year view: 5 of 6 actual exploits caught (83% recall) at 22 patches over 7 years. That is fewer patches AND more catches than NP+DI + AI scan (27 patches, 4 catches, 67% recall).

The synthesis

The 12-month exemplar (Table 1) shows the hacker advantage clearly. NP+DI + AI scan and patch-criticals tie at 67% recall in 2022. Hacker S+A is the only strategy that catches all 3 (Spring4Shell + Text4Shell + SnakeYAML) at fewer patches than patch-all. The Text4Shell and SnakeYAML wins are exactly the deser/utility blind spots the AI scan doesn't currently handle.
The 7-year story (Table 2) confirms it at scale. Hacker S+A: 5/6 catches at 22 patches (4.4x oracle). NP+DI + AI scan: 4/6 catches at 27 patches (6.8x oracle). The hacker beats the comparator on both axes — more catches AND fewer patches. Adding NP+DI to the hacker (union) gets to 100% recall at 37 patches, picking up the only event hacker S+A misses (Tomcat CGI Windows, which NP+DI does catch via CWE-78).

Counter-argument worth holding

  • The hacker exercise is a methodology validation, not a deployable product. Running an LLM-based hacker persona on every CVE batch is expensive and prompt-design-sensitive. The operational artifact is an extended AI scan that encodes the hacker's discriminator as programmatic checks: package-role-aware NP for utility libraries (commons-text, snakeyaml), CWE-502 deserialization promotion on network-facing components, auth-missing CWE co-tag rule. That extension would close the 1-event gap between current AI scan (4/6) and Hacker S+A (5/6) plus the 1 hacker-miss (Tomcat CGI Windows) by going to the union.
  • 6 exploited events is small N. The Hacker 5/6 vs comparator 4/6 gap is meaningful but flip 1 event and headline numbers shift ~17pp.
  • Right-censoring affects 2024-2026 events. If recent CVEs accumulate exploitation evidence by 2027-2028, every strategy's recall improves; relative ordering should hold but absolute numbers shift.
  • Manifest scope changes the story. If the actual deployed estate adds packages we excluded (log4j, XStream, ActiveMQ broker/client), the exploitation base grows but so does the manifest universe — and the relative strategy ordering should hold. We've now seen this manifest sensitivity directly: the apparent 45% NP+DI recall on the inflated 175-event manifest was largely an artifact of including log4j and XStream that weren't actually deployed.

NP+DI vs EPSS vs Hacker Persona — efficiency view

Patches per strategy across one window vs seven windows — does the filter survive scaling out, and at what patch cost?

How we got here

  1. The 12-month synthetic backtest worked. NP+DI alone caught 5 of 8 in-scope exploited CVEs at day zero against a four-ecosystem manifest. EPSS ≥ 0.10 caught 1 of 8 on day one and didn't reach 8/8 until "eventually."
  2. An AI safety-net scan closed the structural gap. The 3 NP+DI misses were all CWE-misclassification cases (Ghostcat tagged CWE-269 instead of CWE-22; ActiveMQ Jolokia tagged CWE-20 instead of CWE-94; Tomcat partial PUT tagged CWE-44+502 instead of being recognized as a chained primitive). An automated CWE-validation pass on NP-but-not-DI advisories catches all 3 on day zero. NP+DI + AI scan: 8/8 with the same speed advantage over EPSS.
  3. We extended the backtest to seven years across multiple frameworks (Spring/Java + Django/Python) to test whether the 12-month "perfect catch" framing survives at scale. Combined exploited universe: 15 events from 356 critical/high CVEs.
  4. An adversarial hacker persona, blind to actual exploitation outcomes, ranked vulnerabilities by attack value (S = drop everything, A = strong campaign candidate, B/C/D = lower priority). The hacker's discriminator was default-config × network-edge × primitive-direct — derived independently from bug shape, not from CWE-set membership.
  5. Hacker S+A turned out to be both the most efficient (fewest unnecessary patches) and safest (fewest missed exploits) review methodology. 14 of 15 actual exploits caught (93% recall) at 67 patches over 7 years — vs NP+DI's 7/15 at 66 patches, criticals-only's 10/15 at 74 patches.

The synthesis

Hacker S+A is the most efficient AND safest review methodology at 7-year scale. Lowest patches-per-exploit (4.79x oracle), highest single-strategy recall (93% — 14 of 15 actual exploits caught). The single miss is CVE-2019-0232 (Tomcat CGI on Windows) — both NP+DI catches it (CWE-78 + Tomcat NP) so the union NP+DI ∪ Hacker-S+A reaches 100% recall at 6.93x oracle.
Per-strategy headlines, 7-year totals (15 exploits over 356 events):
  • Patch all C+H — 356 patches, 100% recall, 23.7x oracle. Maximum patch effort, baseline for "do nothing smart."
  • Patch criticals only — 74 patches, 67% recall, 7.4x oracle. The blunt-instrument default for many security teams. Catches 10 of 15.
  • NP+DI — 66 patches, 47% recall, 9.4x oracle. Worse than criticals on both axes over 7 years — flags slightly fewer events but catches significantly fewer exploits.
  • NP+DI + AI scan — 69 patches, 67% recall, 6.9x oracle. Reaches criticals-level recall with fewer patches.
  • Hacker S+A — 67 patches, 93% recall, 4.79x oracle. Best on both axes simultaneously.
  • NP+DI ∪ Hacker S+A — 104 patches, 100% recall, 6.93x oracle. The cost of the last 7% recall is 37 extra patches.

Counter-argument worth holding

  • The hacker exercise is a methodology validation, not a deployable product. Running an LLM-based hacker persona on every CVE batch is expensive and prompt-design-sensitive. The operational artifact is an AI scan extended to encode the hacker's discriminator as programmatic checks (auth-missing CWE co-tag rule, package-role-aware NP for utility libraries, CWE-502 deserialization promotion on network-facing components, native-C-library memory corruption flagging). That extension would close the 4-event gap between current AI scan (10/15) and Hacker S+A (14/15).
  • Right-censoring affects 2024-2026 events. If recent CVEs accumulate exploitation evidence by 2027-2028, every strategy's recall will improve; the relative ordering should hold but absolute numbers shift.
  • 15 exploited events is small N. Hacker S+A's 14/15 vs NP+DI's 7/15 is a meaningful gap but not statistically conclusive — flip 2-3 events on the boundaries and the headline numbers move ~5pp. The directional finding (hacker S+A > NP+DI alone in both recall and efficiency) is robust; the precise multipliers are wide.
  • The combined Spring + Django universe mixes ecosystems. Java alone (175 events, 11 exploits) shows NP+DI at 5/11 = 45% recall — the same shape as the combined view but slightly different proportions. The Python contribution to combined exploitation (4 events, 0 in NP+DI scope as currently defined except for Django _connector and paramiko widened-DI) is what drives Hacker S+A's relative advantage. Pure-Python operators may see a different efficiency curve.

Operational reading

Three tiers of confidence the data supports:

  • Tier 1 — Drop everything (Hacker S): 5 events across 7 years; 100% precision both rounds. These are the unconditional one-shot RCEs (Log4j, Spring4Shell-class, ActiveMQ OpenWire-class).
  • Tier 2 — This week (Hacker S+A union with NP+DI): ~104 events across 7 years; 100% recall on actual exploitation. The operational policy is the union, not either alone.
  • Tier 3 — Regular cadence: everything else; 0 of the 15 exploits in this set landed below A-tier in either filter.
